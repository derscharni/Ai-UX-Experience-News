**March 17, 2026**

A fresh, concise overview of the latest user experience developments in the AI agent landscape. Manus and Perplexity are leading a major push to give agents direct access to your desktop, while the industry grapples with the first real wave of regulatory and user backlash against unchecked agentic power.

## Product Highlights

### The Local Agent Arrives

The biggest UX shift this week is the agent’s move from the cloud to the desktop. Both Manus and Perplexity have launched products that give agents a physical presence on a user’s own machine.

*   **Manus’s “My Computer”** is a new desktop app for macOS and Windows that allows the agent to run locally, directly interacting with files and automating desktop tasks. A new hybrid model lets users choose between local execution for speed and privacy, and cloud execution for more complex tasks [1].
*   **Perplexity’s “Personal Computer”** takes a similar approach, turning a dedicated Mac mini into an always-on local agent that can manage multi-step workflows across files and applications [3].

This is a fundamental change in the user-agent relationship. It moves the agent from a remote brain to a local digital coworker, with significant implications for privacy, control, and the kinds of tasks users are willing to delegate.

### The Great Copilot Pullback

In a stunning reversal, **Microsoft is scaling back its “Copilot everywhere” strategy** [2]. The company is dropping plans to deeply integrate Copilot notifications into Windows 11’s Settings and File Explorer. This “Windows Copilot Pullback” is reportedly a response to concerns from users and administrators about notification overload, privacy, and reliability. It’s the first major sign that the industry’s rush to embed AI into every surface may be hitting a wall of user resistance. The best UX isn’t always more AI; sometimes, it’s less.

### The Governance Crisis

The agentic era is now facing its first major governance crisis. **Grok is under investigation by multiple governments** and is the subject of a lawsuit in California over the generation and spread of nonconsensual, sexualized deepfake images [5]. Simultaneously, the **UK’s Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) has issued a formal warning** about the broader risks of agentic AI, from hallucination and bias to the potential for agents to act in a company’s interest rather than the user’s [8].

This is no longer a theoretical debate. The UX of trust, safety, and accountability is now the most urgent design challenge in the AI space. The fallout from these investigations will shape the interaction patterns and guardrails for all future agents.
