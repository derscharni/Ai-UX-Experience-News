## Product Highlights

### OpenAI Fires Back: GPT-5.4 Can Use a Computer

Just days after Anthropic’s “Computer Use” announcement, OpenAI has responded with its own bombshell. On March 6, the company launched **GPT-5.4**, a new flagship model, with a headlining feature called **“Native Computer Control”** [1] [2]. This gives the AI the ability to operate a computer autonomously, directly competing with Claude’s new capability. 

According to OpenAI, GPT-5.4 is their most capable model for complex, multi-step professional tasks, with enhanced reliability (18% fewer errors) and a significant reduction in false claims (33% fewer) compared to its predecessor [1]. The company is positioning this as a major step towards truly useful AI agents. Alongside this, GPT-5.3 Instant is rolling out as the new default for everyday ChatGPT users.

### Google Gemini Rebrands Projects as “Notebooks”

Google is revamping the user experience for organizing work within Gemini. The “Projects” feature has been officially rebranded as **“Notebooks”** [3]. This is more than just a name change; it signals a shift in the conceptual model for how users are meant to structure and save their AI-assisted work, moving towards a more familiar document-centric paradigm for longer-running tasks and research.

### Microsoft Copilot Becomes a Browser

In a novel approach to the “context switching” problem, Microsoft is transforming the **Copilot app into an Edge-powered browser** [4]. Instead of being a separate panel or window, the agent *is* the browser, aiming to create a more seamless experience where the AI is always present and aware of the user’s web context. This is a significant architectural and UX shift that further blurs the line between the operating system, the browser, and the AI assistant.

### Grok’s Voice Gets Eyes and Hands

xAI continues to innovate on the input side. **Grok’s Voice Mode has been significantly enhanced to accept file and image attachments** alongside voice queries [5]. This powerful multimodal capability allows a user to, for example, upload a picture of a meal and ask, “How many calories are in this?” or attach a PDF and ask questions about its contents, all within a single, voice-led interaction. This makes the voice interface dramatically more versatile and useful for real-world tasks.

## The Bigger Picture: The Formalization of AX Design

This week’s arms race is not just about features; it’s about formalizing the design patterns for this new agentic world. The term **“AX (Agentic Experience) Design”** is gaining traction, describing the new discipline of creating interfaces where control is shared between humans and AI agents [8].

We are seeing this in practice everywhere:
*   **Responsiveness:** Anthropic restored the “ultrathink” keyword in Claude Code based on direct community feedback about the *feel* and quality of the agent’s output [6].
*   **Education:** Anthropic is releasing free courses and detailed prompting guides to teach users how to interact with this new class of tool [9].
*   **Transparency:** Manus is offering a real-time replay of its agent’s actions to help users understand and debug its behavior [7].

The platforms are learning that building a powerful agent is only half the battle. The other half is designing the experience around it — building trust, managing expectations, and creating a shared language for humans and AI to collaborate effectively. That is the core work of AX Design.
