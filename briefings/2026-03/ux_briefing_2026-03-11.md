## Product Highlights

### The Agent Can Buy Things. Should It? Amazon vs. Perplexity

The biggest news of the day is a legal earthquake. **Amazon has won a court order blocking Perplexity’s “Comet” agentic browser from making purchases on its site** [1]. This is the first major legal challenge to the concept of autonomous AI commerce, and it sets a critical precedent. Perplexity’s agent was designed to automate the entire shopping process for users, but Amazon successfully argued that this violated its terms of service. The ruling raises fundamental UX and legal questions: Who is the user? The human, or the agent acting on their behalf? How do we design for consent, accountability, and error correction when the user is not the one clicking the “buy” button? This case will shape the design of agentic commerce for years to come.

### The Agent Is the Browser: Manus Joins the Fray

Just as the legal boundaries are being drawn, the technology is pushing forward. **Manus has launched its own agentic browser**, allowing its AI to operate directly within a user’s open browser tabs [2]. Like Perplexity’s Comet, it can mimic human interaction to perform multi-step tasks like researching flights, comparing products, and completing purchases on authenticated sites. This represents a fundamental shift toward an **agent-first UI**, where the AI is not a separate application but a layer of intelligence integrated directly into the user’s existing web environment.

### The Great Integration: Agents Invade the Office Suite

While the agentic browser wars heat up, the productivity giants are executing a different strategy: deep integration. 

*   **Microsoft’s “Wave 3” for M365 Copilot** is a massive update that allows users to build their own custom AI agents for specific workflows directly within apps like Word and Excel [3]. It also introduces “Work IQ,” an intelligence layer that gives the agent a deeper understanding of organizational context.
*   **Google** is rolling out new Gemini-powered features across its Workspace suite, including a “Help me create” tool in Docs that can generate entire first drafts of documents [6].
*   **Zoom** has launched **Zoom Workplace**, a major redesign centered on a dedicated AI Companion tab and new AI-first canvases (Docs, Sheets, Slides) for real-time co-creation directly within meetings [5].

This is the quiet revolution. While headline-grabbing agents are learning to use computers, the tools we use every day are being rebuilt from the ground up with agents at their core.

## The Bigger Picture: Show, Don’t Just Tell

Two other updates this week point to a maturing approach to agentic UX. First, **ChatGPT can now generate interactive visuals** for math and science concepts [4]. Instead of just describing a formula, it can create a dynamic chart where a user can manipulate variables and see the outcome. This is a powerful new output modality that moves beyond text to a more intuitive, explorable form of explanation.

Second, Anthropic has quietly published its own internal workflow document, **`CLAUDE.md`** [7]. This file, likely used to train and guide its own agents, offers an unprecedented look at the company’s own thinking on interaction design, prompt structure, and agentic behavior. In an industry full of press releases, this is a rare artifact of genuine design practice. It’s a move toward transparency and a recognition that the best way to teach users how to interact with agents is to show them how the experts do it.
