## Product Highlights

### Claude: Designing in Figma

In a significant development for design workflows, Claude can now **design directly within Figma** as of February 27 [1]. This new interaction pattern allows users to go from a natural language prompt to a vector prototype inside the design tool, dramatically accelerating the early stages of UI/UX design. This move follows a similar capability announced by Google for Gemini and signals a major new battleground for agentic AI: creative tools.

### Microsoft Copilot: Accessing Google Contacts

Microsoft has enabled a surprisingly deep integration with its main competitor. As of February 28, **Copilot can now access a user's Google Contacts**, a significant improvement for productivity workflows that span both ecosystems [2]. This allows Copilot to assist with tasks like composing emails or scheduling meetings with contacts stored in a user's Google account, reducing friction and context switching. This is a notable example of user experience needs trumping platform-level competition.

### Google Gemini: Image Quality Praised, Model Stability Questioned

Google’s new **Nano Banana 2 image model has taken the #1 position** in the community-run Artificial Analysis Image Arena, praised for its quality and speed [3]. However, this success is tempered by the emergence of **critical feedback on the underlying Gemini 3.1 model**, with developers citing increased instability and a perceived downgrade in the Agent Manager UX [4]. This highlights the ongoing tension between shipping cutting-edge features and maintaining a stable, predictable user experience.

### Grok: Improved Dialogue and Broader Language Support

xAI released **Grok 4.1** on February 27, with a focus on more natural and fluid conversational dialogue [5]. On February 28, the platform also added support for five additional European languages (Italian, German, French, Portuguese, and Spanish), significantly expanding its accessibility and international user base [6].

### The Bigger Picture: Designing for Agents

The most significant trend this week is the crystallization of a new design paradigm, articulated in a widely-circulated article on *uxdesign.cc*: **interfaces must now be designed for AI agents as the primary user** [10].

> The argument is that as humans increasingly delegate tasks by stating high-level objectives, the primary "user" of an application's interface will be the AI agent executing the task. This requires a fundamental shift in UX design, moving away from optimizing for human visual perception and motor skills towards creating interfaces that are easily navigable, predictable, and understandable for autonomous systems.

This trend is visible in Google's "AppFunctions," Microsoft's auditable CUA framework, and the core concept of Perplexity Computer. The user experience of the future may be less about what the human sees and clicks, and more about how effectively they can direct an agent that is seeing and clicking on their behalf.
