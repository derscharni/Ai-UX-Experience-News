Both changes address the same underlying UX need: when an agent has broad access, it must also have narrow blast radius — and worktree isolation is a filesystem-level expression of that principle.

---

What all six products signal this week is that **permission design is becoming the primary differentiator in agentic UX**. Claude's `waitingFor` field, ChatGPT's layered session/agent controls, Gemini's per-query user-feedback loops in generative UI, Microsoft's live guardrail demo, Grok Build's visible-and-killable monitors, and Perplexity's mid-task privacy escalation are all answering the same question differently: *at which moment do you ask the human, and for exactly what?* The industry is no longer debating whether agents should require permission — every platform now agrees they should. The design competition has moved to the interaction pattern of the gate itself: silent-until-sensitive, layered by scope, and recoverable after the fact.
