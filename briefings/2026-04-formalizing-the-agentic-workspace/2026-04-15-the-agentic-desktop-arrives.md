**April 15, 2026**

Good morning. Today's briefing highlights a massive shift from the browser to the desktop. The biggest players in the space are rolling out native applications designed to operate directly on your local machine, bringing agents out of the chat window and into your file system.

## Product Highlights

### The Race to the Desktop

The era of the browser-bound chatbot is ending. Both Anthropic and Manus AI made major moves this week to embed their agents directly into the user's operating system. 

Anthropic completely redesigned its Claude Code desktop app. [1] The new UX is a dense, developer-focused dashboard featuring an integrated terminal, an in-app file editor, a diff viewer, and rearrangeable panes. Crucially, they added a "side chat" feature, allowing users to ask questions without interrupting the agent's ongoing autonomous work. They also introduced "routines," enabling scheduled or trigger-driven execution of tasks in the background.

Simultaneously, Manus AI launched its new desktop application featuring "My Computer." [2] This allows the Manus agent to operate directly on the local machine, interacting with files and system operations just as a human user would. This is a profound shift in interaction design: the agent is no longer a tool you talk to; it is a co-worker operating the same machine you are.

### E-Commerce and Visual Collaboration in Chat

OpenAI is pushing the boundaries of what can be accomplished inside a single chat thread. ChatGPT introduced "Instant Checkout," allowing users to purchase products directly within the chat interface without being redirected to an external website. [5] This turns the conversational interface into a point of sale.

Furthermore, a new integration with Lucid enables visual collaboration directly within ChatGPT. [6] Users can now generate and manipulate visual assets (like diagrams or flowcharts) in real-time alongside the text conversation, moving the UX from pure text to multimodal collaboration.

### Skills and Reusability

A clear UX pattern is emerging across multiple platforms: the need for reusable, customized instructions. Google is rolling out a "Skills" feature in Chrome, allowing users to save and reuse Gemini prompts that sync across devices. [3] Similarly, a leaked update for Grok 5 revealed a new "Skills" feature that lets users create customized instruction sets for specific tasks. [8] This aligns with Claude's recent introduction of a built-in Skill Creator, showing that power users demand the ability to codify and automate their most frequent interactions rather than typing them out from scratch every time.
