# UX Briefing: The UI Retreats, The Agent Advances

**April 11, 2026**

Good morning. Today's briefing highlights a fascinating contradiction in agent UX: as the underlying models become vastly more capable of complex, autonomous execution, the user interfaces are actually retreating. We are seeing a shift away from omnipresent "chat buttons" everywhere, moving toward centralized execution engines, ambient voice, and focused, high-value integration.

## Product Highlights

### The Copilot Retreat

The most interesting UX trend right now is Microsoft's strategic retreat of Copilot. [1] After a year of embedding the Copilot button into every conceivable surface, the 2026 roadmap shows Microsoft dialing it back. They are removing Copilot integrations from Windows 11 apps like Notepad and Snipping Tool, creating a cleaner Widgets panel, and even removing Copilot from M365 apps (Word, Excel) for certain enterprise tiers unless an add-on is purchased. [2] 

This reflects a maturation in UX philosophy: users don't want a chatbot interrupting every micro-task. They want the agent available for heavy lifting, but otherwise out of the way.

### Gemini's Floating Overlay and 3D Output

Google is also refining its interface footprint. Gemini on Android is receiving a visual redesign that replaces the heavy fullscreen interface with a lighter, floating overlay. [3] This allows users to maintain context of the app they are currently using while interacting with the agent. 

Simultaneously, Gemini is expanding its output capabilities. It can now generate interactive 3D models, charts, and simulations directly within the chat response. [4] This transforms the chat window from a text feed into a dynamic, interactive canvas.

### Pricing the Autonomous Agent

As agents shift from "answering questions" to "executing projects," the UX of *paying* for them is changing. Perplexity's new "Computer" agent—which orchestrates 19 different models to execute multi-step workflows—has introduced a usage-based pricing model. [9] Instead of a flat monthly subscription, users purchase credits. This aligns the UX of billing with the reality of compute costs for autonomous execution, treating the agent more like cloud infrastructure than a consumer app.

## Broader Trends: Desktop Integration

The race to the desktop continues. Claude Cowork is now generally available on macOS and Windows through the Claude Desktop app. [7] By moving out of the browser and onto the local machine, agents reduce friction. Users no longer have to upload files or copy-paste context; the agent lives alongside the work, setting the stage for deeper "My Computer" style integrations across the industry.
